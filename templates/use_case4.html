Fraud Detection in Car Sales

Identifying suspicious listings and anomalous seller behaviour in online automotive marketplaces without relying on labelled fraud examples.

Problem Description

The Challenge

Online car marketplaces face a growing volume of fraudulent listings: vehicles advertised with manipulated odometer readings, misrepresented accident history, inflated or deliberately underpriced listings designed to attract victims, and accounts exhibiting patterns consistent with scam operations.

Unlike supervised fraud detection, labelled fraud datasets are rarely available in this domain — fraud is underreported and evolves over time. Unsupervised anomaly detection learns the statistical distribution of legitimate listings and flags records that deviate significantly from expected patterns, without requiring prior examples of fraud.

Type of ML

Unsupervised Learning — Anomaly Detection. Models such as Isolation Forest and Autoencoders learn the "normal" data distribution and assign anomaly scores to new listings without requiring labelled fraud examples.

Input Features

  • Listed price vs. market median
  • Mileage vs. age ratio
  • Number of listings per seller
  • Listing age and edit frequency
  • Geographic inconsistencies
  • Description quality score
  • Image hash (duplicate detection)

Expected Output

An anomaly score between 0 and 1 per listing, plus a binary suspicious / normal flag when the score exceeds a calibrated threshold. High-scoring listings are queued for human review.

Real-World Relevance

Protects buyers from financial loss, reduces manual moderation overhead for platform operators, and increases marketplace trust — a key driver of long-term user retention and revenue.

Technical Notes

Algorithms & Evaluation Challenges

The primary algorithm used is Isolation Forest, which isolates anomalies by recursively partitioning the feature space. Anomalies are points that require fewer partitions to isolate — they are statistically "easy to separate" from the bulk of the data.

Evaluating unsupervised models is inherently difficult without ground-truth labels. Proxy evaluation uses Precision@k (the fraction of the top-k flagged listings that are confirmed fraudulent by manual review) and recall on a small labelled validation set assembled from confirmed past cases.

Threshold selection involves a precision-recall trade-off: a stricter threshold reduces false positives (legitimate listings wrongly flagged) but increases false negatives (fraud that goes undetected).
